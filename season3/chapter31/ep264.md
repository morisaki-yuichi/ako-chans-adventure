The boy bends over the table and waits. The low hum fills the quiet greenhouse. Behind the two closed eyes, the blue glow grows. It is brighter now, steady and clear. He does not move. He hardly breathes. Beside him, the red light waits too.

Then the eyes open. They open slowly, and they shine. The light is pale blue, almost white, deep and cool. The two small points glow in the dim room. And then they turn. They find the boy's face and hold it there. The eyes look straight at him, and do not look away. For all his life, every human face has been a gray light. Animals have looked at him with their own clear eyes. But this is neither. This look is new. The thing he made is looking at him, and it sees him.

The boy cannot move. He stands locked in that pale blue gaze. The hum runs on, low and even, under the silence. The lamp burns small on the table beside them. The red light is close at his side, very still. Outside, the spring morning is bright and cold. A thin wind moves against the glass walls. Nothing in the room makes a sound but the hum.

Something turns over deep inside the boy. He has made things before — small things, dead things. None of them ever looked back at him. This one does. The pale blue eyes stay on his face, calm and clear and waiting. He does not know what they want. He does not know what he feels. But he knows that he has been seen — seen for the first time by something he made.
