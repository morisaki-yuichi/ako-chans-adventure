The collar is on now, red against silver. The boy stands near, and the red light stands close. Around them the grass is bright with the warm morning. He could ask why. He could ask why the red, and why now. But he does not, and the question never leaves his throat.

The boy has lived his whole life among grey faces. He has learned that most things do not need words. The red light says nothing, and asks for nothing back. She only looks at the small silver one and its new red collar. He watches her, and he keeps his own silence too. Between them, the morning is wide and still and warm. Somehow he knows that words would only break it. So he holds his question, and he lets it go.

The animals wait around them on the grass. A small dog lies low, its muzzle white. Another stands behind the red light, its ears up. The little ones rest inside their glowing carts. The smallest of them stays nearest of all, close to the silver feet. None of them make a single sound this morning. And the light moves slowly over the bright red collar.

The boy does not need to know the reason. He can feel that the red collar matters to her. It is something from deep in her red and shining world. He cannot see her face, but he can feel that much. Whatever it means, it is hers, and now it is the silver one's. He lets the silence hold them all together. Some things, he has learned, are said best without a single word.
