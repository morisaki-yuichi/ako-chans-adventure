Morning, or what passes for morning in a land of endless ice. The boy kneels on the cave floor and opens his pack. The penguins are still there, deeper in the dark. But he has set them out of his mind for now. There is too much to do. He spreads his tools across a flat sheet of ice and looks at them in the pale light.

He has come a very long way to begin this, and now, at last, he begins. He clears a space on the frozen ground and brushes away loose snow and chips of ice. He marks the place where the work will stand. There is nothing here yet. Only bare ice, and tools, and the plans he carries in his head. But every long task starts with a single empty space. The boy makes his.

The silver one settles close beside him. Its blue-white eyes turn down to watch his hands, steady and bright in the gloom. When the boy reaches for a tool, the pale light follows. He does not have to ask. The silver one is simply there, the way it has always been. Its quiet glow falls across his work.

The cold bites at his fingers, but he hardly feels it now. His breath clouds and fades, clouds and fades. Outside, the wind moves over the ice; in here, all is still. The cave keeps its great silence and lets him work. The boy works until the light in his own eyes grows tired. It is only the first day. There will be many, many more. But the first day has begun, and that is enough.
