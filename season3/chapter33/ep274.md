The boy walks deeper into the cave. The silver one goes ahead, its pale eyes lighting the way. The blue ice glows around them, cold and clear. And there, at the edge of the light, the two shapes become real. They are birds. Two penguins, standing on the ice, very still.

One is tall, taller than the boy expected. It has a smooth dark head and a pale gold throat. The other is smaller, black above and white below, with a thin white ring around each eye. They do not run. They do not make a sound. They stand side by side on the old ice and face the opening. They look as if they have waited there a long time.

The boy stops a few steps away. He has seen penguins before, in pictures. He knows they live in places like this, far in the cold south. To him, these are only two birds of the ice. Wild things that happened to be here when he came. He wonders, for a moment, why they do not flee. Most wild animals run from people. These two do not move at all.

The silver one stands beside him, its blue-white eyes fixed on the birds. The cave is silent. Somewhere far off, the ice creaks and settles. The boy's breath drifts white in the cold air. The frozen walls catch the glow and hold it, so the whole cave seems lit from within. He looks at the two penguins. And the two penguins look past him — at the small silver shape at his side. The boy sees nothing strange in that. They are only penguins, he thinks. Only birds, in a place full of ice.
