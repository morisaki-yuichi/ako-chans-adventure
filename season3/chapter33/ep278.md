Night settles into the ice cave. The boy sleeps by his tools, deep inside his coat. Beside him rests the small silver one. Its blue-white eyes glow low, like a lamp before sleep. The glow lies soft on the old blue ice. At the far wall stand two birds, side by side. All day, neither of them has spoken.

Pen-san breaks the long silence first. "You saw it too," he says, very low.

"I saw it," says the General.

"The collar. The eyes. The sound when it walks."

"Yes."

"It is the friend," says Pen-san. His voice shakes once, then holds. "It came back to us."

The General is quiet for a while. "It does not know us," he says at last. "It looked at me, and nothing moved. Ice looks at us that way."

"Not yet," says Pen-san. "It does not know us yet."

They do not try to understand how this can be. The thought is too big, and they let it stand. Once, in another life, the two of them crossed the white land beside that collar. The cave ticks and creaks around them, slow and old. The boy's breath rises and falls, small in the great dark. The blue-white glow dims and brightens, then dims again. The two birds keep their voices low, out of old habit. No one in this cave could understand them anyway. They lower their voices all the same.

"What do we do?" asks Pen-san.

The General looks at the sleeping boy. Then he looks at the soft blue glow. "We stay," he says. "We watch."

"Yes. Like before."

The knowing is still heavy in them both. But a weight grows lighter when two stand under it. The two old soldiers settle into the dark, side by side. In the deep south, in the ice, a new watch begins.
