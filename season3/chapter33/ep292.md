General Adelie holds his place on the ice and says nothing at all. He is small beside the great Emperor, no taller than a man's knee. A white ring circles each dark eye. His back is straight. His flippers are pressed to his sides. He stands the way a soldier stands. So he has stood through every cold year of his long life.

Once, long ago, he did not trust the silver one. He was a Colonel then, sharp and stern, and he marked the strange traveller with hard, careful eyes. Nothing it did escaped him. He waited for it to prove false. It never did. In time the Colonel became a General, and the suspicion in him turned to something quieter and deeper.

Now the General watches again. But this watching is not the old watching. There is no doubt in it, no warning, no cold command. He looks at the small silver shape with its blue-white eyes. What he understands now, he can never show. Here stands a friend from a journey not yet made, a friend who has not learned their faces. The General has no words for such a thing. So he offers none.

He simply stands his post. The ice rings faintly in the dark, a thin sound that fades and returns. The boy works on. The far walls throw the small sounds back, thin and broken, until they fade. Cold air pools low along the floor and does not stir. Pen-san keeps his place. And the General keeps his, straight and still. His silence says what his voice cannot. It settles over the cave and watches the long, strange hours go by.
