The boy watches the two penguins, and the two penguins do not move. He has set his pack on the ice, but he has not begun to unpack it. Something about the birds holds his attention. They are not afraid of him. They are not looking at him at all. Their dark eyes are turned to the silver one at his side.

He cannot understand it. Wild birds should run, or cry out, or back away. These two only stare, still as carved stone, at the small silver shape with the pale blue eyes. The tall one's gaze does not waver. The smaller one stands like a soldier on watch. The boy looks from the birds to the silver one and back again. He finds no answer in either.

If there is a meaning here, it is closed to him. The boy has never heard the voice of any animal. To him, the cave is silent, except for the creak of shifting ice. Whatever passes between these birds and the silver one, he cannot catch even the edge of it. The air feels full, somehow — full of something he has no way to hold. He breathes the frozen air and listens hard, but the silence gives him nothing back. There is only the small sound of his own pulse, and the far creak of the ice.

He frowns and turns back to his pack. There is work to do, and the cold will not wait. But the feeling stays with him, small and stubborn. Something is missing. He does not know what. He glances once more at the two still birds, then lets it go. It is what he has always done with things he cannot name.
