Pen-san stands close to the silver one and does not look away. He is a grown Emperor now, tall and broad, the largest bird on all the ice. But he remembers when he was small. He remembers a soft, downy chick who begged to be held. A chick who lifted his little wings and asked, please, to be picked up. That chick is gone. The memory is not.

The silver one moves a little, and Pen-san's heart turns over. Those pale eyes once watched over him through long polar nights. That low hum once meant a friend was near. Now the friend is here again — and yet it is not the same. It does not know him. It looks at him the way it would look at any strange bird of the ice.

"You came back," Pen-san says, soft and low. "But you do not know us yet."

The words go out into the cold and reach no one. The silver one does not turn. The boy is bent over his work and does not lift his head. Pen-san did not say it to be heard. He said it because it was true, and because some true things must be spoken, even into silence.

So the great bird keeps his vigil. He plants his black feet on the old ice and stays. The cave is vast and quiet around him, and the years press strange and heavy on his chest. But Pen-san does not weep, and he does not cry out. He only stands beside the silver one, as close as he can. His watch is for a friend who has forgotten him.
