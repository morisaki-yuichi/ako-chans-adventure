The morning is too quiet. Even the wind has stopped. Out on the shore ice, the boy cuts blocks of snow, the silver one bright beside him. Deep in the cave mouth, Pen-san lifts his head. He knows this stillness. It is the breath before the white wind.

The storm comes fast, the way it always comes. The sky goes white, and then the white begins to roar. Snow lifts off the ground and flies sideways. The boy cannot see the cave. He cannot see his own way back. He turns one way, then another, blind in the howling white. The silver one presses close against his legs.

Then a great dark shape is beside them. Pen-san. He leans his whole body against the boy and pushes him along, step by step. Behind them comes the General, small and hard as a stone, herding them on. The birds do not stop. They know this white. Long ago, on a night like this, a friend walked out into it alone. They remember what the white wind takes. "We have done this before," calls Pen-san over the roar. "...Yes," says the General. "For the friend." The words fly away, unheard by anyone.

The cave mouth opens black in the white, and they fall inside. The roar drops away behind them. The boy sits on the floor, shaking, snow in every fold of his coat. The two birds shake the storm from their feathers and stand calm.

By morning the white wind has gone. The boy steps out into a world made smooth and new. He looks at the two birds for a long time. Wild birds do not do what these two did. He has no way to ask, and they have no way to tell. But that night, he lays his sleeping place a little nearer to theirs. The General watches him do it, and says nothing. Pen-san's eyes are soft in the pale light.
