The days in the cave have found their shape. The boy works at the cleared blue floor, hour after hour. The small silver one keeps its light upon his hands. And slowly, quietly, the two birds have begun to move.

Each day the boy goes out for snow to melt, and the silver one goes with him. The shore ice is old and full of hidden cracks. The first time, Pen-san simply stands up and walks ahead of them. He does not look back. He walks a long, bent path across the white, and the boy follows the big bird without thinking. The path holds. Where the ice sings thin, General Adelie is already standing on it. He waits there, square and still, like a small black gate. They go around him. "It does not understand us," says the General. "It follows," says Pen-san. "That is enough, for now."

The days repeat, and the lessons repeat with them. The birds walk ahead; the boy and the silver one follow. The boy thinks the penguins are only going the same way. He does not guess that a path is being given to him. But the silver one has begun to watch the birds. Its pale eyes follow Pen-san a little longer each day. When the General stands guard on thin ice, the blue-white gaze rests on him, and stays.

Once, long ago, on white land like this, that silver shape walked out alone into a storm. It went so that others would be safe. The two birds have not forgotten. Now they walk ahead, and keep the way safe for it. The care goes back the other way now. Neither bird says so. They only walk, and watch, and lead.
