Deeper in the cave, the ice is older than anything the boy has ever touched. It is not white here but a deep, glassy blue, packed hard by the weight of ages. When he lays his hand flat against it, the cold climbs into his bones. This is the floor he will build upon. First, he must make it ready.

The boy works across the frozen ground without haste. He clears the loose snow and levels the rough places with care. Where the ice rises in ridges, he smooths it down. Where it dips, he fills it. There is no machine here, no frame, no shape of any kind. The work is only this: to make an empty place ready. The thing he means to build will come later, over years he cannot yet count.

The cave speaks while he works. The old ice creaks and groans, settling under its own great weight. Now and then a far crack rings out, sharp as breaking glass, and rolls away into the dark. His breath hangs white in the air. The silver one's pale light pools on the blue floor and shows him where to put his hands. The penguins watch from the shadows, two still shapes at the edge of the glow. They have not moved since he began, and they do not move now.

By the time the boy stops, the place is smooth and bare. Nothing stands on it yet. But it is ready now. He looks at the cleared ice for a long moment, then lets out a slow breath. The first ground is laid. The rest is still ahead, somewhere in the long years to come.
