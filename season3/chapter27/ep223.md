The afternoon is fading over the winter park. The boy sits in his place and watches. Across the grass, the red light sits with her animals. To him, the people passing are only grey. But he is not watching the people today. He is watching her, and something strange is happening.

High on a bare branch, a crow tips its head. The red light looks up at it and smiles. Then she laughs — soft, and warm, and sudden. At nothing. No one has spoken. Nothing has moved. The boy leans forward on his cold bench. A moment later, the small dog, Dango, looks up too. His tail sweeps once across the frost. One of the little carts rolls closer to her feet. She nods down at it, as if answering. The boy watches, very still, from his bench. She is hearing something. Something he cannot hear. He is sure of it now, though it makes no sense.

The dusk gathers slowly between the trees. The lamps wake, one by one, along the far path. The crow drops from its branch and crosses the sky. The cold sharpens as the light goes. The red light rises and gathers her friends. The boy watches them go through the far gate.

That night, the boy lies awake at home. He has never thought so much about a person. People were always grey, and far, and empty. But she laughs at silence, and small things answer her. He turns the strange pieces over in the dark. He wants to understand what she hears. He wants tomorrow to come. Never before has a tomorrow pulled at him like this. He lies in the dark and waits for morning.
