The winter morning is pale and quiet. The boy sits on his bench with a notebook. Its cover is grey, like everything else. He holds a short pencil in bare fingers. The shapes of people pass along the paths. He watches them, and now and then he writes.

He does not write what he thinks. He only writes what the shapes do. A tall shape drops a bottle by the pond and walks on. He writes it down. Another shape pulls its small dog hard away from a smell. The dog's legs slide on the frosted path. He writes that too. Short lines, one under another, in small letters. He has filled many pages this winter. The lines are plain and cold and true. He never shows them to anyone. There is no one to show.

The morning moves slowly over the park. A big crow crosses high above his bench. Its wings beat the still air, and it is gone. Far off, at the park's edge, an old greenhouse stands in the corner. Its glass is dim, and no one goes in. The boy looks at it for a moment, then back at his page. His fingers grow stiff around the pencil. The grey shapes come and go, and none of them look at him.

At last he reads the new lines over once. Then he closes the notebook and puts it away. The bottle still lies by the pond. The small dog is gone. The park empties slowly in the cold. The boy sits a while longer, empty-handed and still. Tomorrow there will be more lines. There always are.
