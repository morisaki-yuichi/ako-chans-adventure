Now the boy comes to the park every day. He sits on the same hard bench and waits. Most days, the red light comes too. It crosses the park with its small dog. The three little carts roll close behind. The boy knows the hour it will come. When it does not come, the day feels long. When it comes, something in him settles.

From his far bench, he watches them together. He sees the red light bend to the small dog. He sees its hand rest on the dog's back. A reddish dog sits guard a step behind. The little tortoises roll near the red light's feet. He learns them slowly, day by day. He does not cross the wide grass between. He has never walked up to anyone. He would not know how to begin.

On his knee, the grey notebook lies open. But his pencil has stopped moving. In front of the red light, there is nothing he wants to write. One question grows instead, and will not leave. Why is only one light red? Every other person is dull and grey. He has counted them his whole life. But this one burns clear, every single day. He cannot explain it to himself. And still he comes back, and still it is red.

The winter light fades early over the park. The red light turns home through the far gate. The boy watches until the last glow is gone. He has never wished for much. The grey world asked nothing of him. But now he wants one simple thing. He wants to know more about the red. Something in him, at last, is reaching out — towards the one thing that is not grey.
