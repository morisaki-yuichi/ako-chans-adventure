Many days have passed in the winter park now. The boy comes to the same bench each day. He sits at the same hour, in the same spot. The winter light falls at the same low angle. The bare trees throw the same thin shadows. Frost lies white in the morning grass. The frozen air holds the park in place. Everything keeps its quiet, settled shape.

His days have grown around this one hour. He wakes, and he waits for the afternoon. He walks the known road to the park. He sits and watches for the red light. When it comes, his day has its centre. The red moves, and he follows every step. The small dog, the reddish one behind, the carts, the tortoises — he knows them now. He has spoken no word to any of them. He only watches, far off and still. He asks nothing, and gives nothing away. But this hour is the best of his day.

One corner of the bench has worn smooth. It is the place his hand always rests. Dry leaves lie thick around his feet. The same sharp wind moves the bare branches. Dry leaves scrape softly along the path. The red light crosses the grass again. The boy watches it, quiet and unmoving. Everything is just as it always is.

Then something changes, small and slow. One of the little tortoises stops still. Its cart turns from the others, towards him. Slowly, it begins to roll his way. The boy goes very still on his bench. His breath holds in the cold air. Across the cold grass, the small thing comes. And the boy does not move at all.
