Winter holds the park quiet and bright. Ako-chan sits on the bench with her friends. The bare trees stand black against a pale sky. Frost hides all day in the shaded grass. Dango rests his warm chin on her boot. The three Pods glow on the path — red, blue, yellow.

A heavy flap comes down through the cold air. Kara-san lands on the back of the bench. He is the oldest crow in the park, and he carries himself like it. "Cold today," he says, rough and plain. "The ground is hard. Slow worms. But the pond people bring more bread."

"Good morning, Kara-san," says Ako-chan.

"A good one, yes. Quiet sky. Nothing wrong." He turns one bright eye towards the pond. "The ducks will complain. They always do."

From the water, a duck calls, low and flat. "The crow takes too much bread. Every day he takes too much."

Kara-san clicks his beak. "The crow is quick. That is all." A squirrel runs down the frosted trunk and stops. "Mine," it says, quick and high. "My tree. My store. Mine." Then it is gone, up into the branches.

Ako-chan laughs, soft and white in the winter air. She listens with her eyes half closed, the way she has listened since she was small. Every small voice is calm. The park under the pale sky is at peace. Dango's ear turns at the crow, then settles. Suke-san watches from her right, easy and still. Luca's head is out in the thin sun.

Far across the park, the boy sits on his bench. He looks at the same trees, the same birds. But no voice reaches him on the wind. He does not know the park can speak. Two worlds share one quiet afternoon. One is full of voices. The other is silent. And neither one knows the other yet.
