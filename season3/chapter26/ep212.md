The boy comes to the park often. He comes at the same hour each day. He walks the same quiet road to get there. He sits on the same bench by the bare trees. Winter has settled deep over the park. The grass is pale and stiff with frost. A thin wind moves across the open ground.

Here, he is always alone. He does not stand among the people. He does not join them where they gather. The crowds mean little to him anyway. When he cannot see a face, he cannot know who is there. So he stays at the edge, on his bench. He watches the pale shapes move and pass. They drift together and apart again. He keeps his own small place, away from them all.

The shadows of the trees fall long and thin. They stripe the hard ground near his feet. Far off, the murmur of the people rises. Children laugh somewhere beyond the trees. A dog barks once, then falls quiet. The winter wind slips under his collar. He pulls his coat a little closer. A cold mist drifts low over the grass. The far gate opens and closes again. New shapes come in; old ones leave. He does not follow any of them. The afternoon is wide and still around him.

The boy does not feel lonely here. Being alone is not a sad thing to him. It is only how his days have always gone. It is simply where he is, and always was. He sits and breathes the cold, clean air. From far away, he watches the gray crowd. The park goes on without him, and that is fine. He has no wish to be part of it.
