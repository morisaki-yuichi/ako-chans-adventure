The afternoon is cold and still. A boy sits alone on a wooden bench. The winter park is wide and bare around him. The trees have lost all their leaves. A few dry leaves turn on the hard ground. People walk along the paths nearby. They come and go in the low afternoon light. The boy watches them, quiet and unmoving.

He cannot see faces. To him, each one is a dull gray light. He cannot tell one person from another. A man passes with a dog. A woman walks the other way. A child runs by, calling out. But their faces are all the same gray blur. They waver and shift as they move. He cannot hold any of them in his mind. The boy has lived like this since birth. He has never known another way to see. He never tells anyone. There is nothing to tell.

The wind moves through the empty branches. The bench is hard beneath him. A bird calls somewhere far away. The winter light is low and thin. The sky hangs heavy and white above him. The cold creeps slowly through his thin coat. A dog barks far off, then falls quiet. The pale shapes drift past, slow and quiet. Some come close to him. Some stay far across the grass. None of them mean anything at all. They are only shapes, moving and gone.

The boy closes his eyes for a while. The chill air touches his face and hands. But the gray does not leave him. It stays behind his eyes, dim and soft. He opens them and watches the park again. The faceless shapes pass by, on and on. This is his world. He knows no other.
