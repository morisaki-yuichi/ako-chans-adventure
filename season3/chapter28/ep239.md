The crowd moves thick across the park today. People come and go in the fading light. To the boy, they blur into one gray mass. Not one of them stands out from the rest. They never have, in all his quiet years. But one light does not blur with the others.

The red light moves slowly among the crowd. She is one person, clear and bright and whole. The boy's eyes lock on her and hold. While she is near, he cannot look away. The others flow past, around her, behind her. They stay a mass, dull and far. But she is not a mass. She is one. He sees her — truly, fully, one. And so the gray crowd feels less solid now. If one can shine, what of the rest? The boy cannot finish the thought. It is too large, too new. But it will not leave him now.

The cold wind pushes through the thinning trees. The animals rest near her, quiet and sure. The little tortoise glows soft at her feet. The small dog leans warm against her leg. The light grows dim as the day sinks low. The boy sits fixed upon the single red. The trees stand bare against the darkening sky. The grass is cold and flat beneath his feet.

He has no words for what is shifting. He only knows that something has come loose. The world looks the same, and yet does not. A whole world of gray, and one red light. The one does not erase the many. But it cracks them, somewhere, just a little. A single light, against so many. The boy watches the red move through the dim. One light, he feels, is changing everything.
