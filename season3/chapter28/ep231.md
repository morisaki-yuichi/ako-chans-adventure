The afternoon light is thin and pale. The boy sits on his cold bench and watches. Across the grass, the red light moves slowly. The small dog walks close at her side. Today his eyes are on the dog, not her. He watches how it keeps beside the red light.

The small dog stays near her feet, always. It does not run ahead, or fall behind. When she slows, the dog slows too. When she stops, the dog stops at once. It does not look up to check her face. It seems to know just where she is. It feels her there, the way you feel warmth. The space between them never changes. It is not too wide, and not too close. The dog holds that one place, step by step. The boy watches, and something puzzles him. How does it know, without ever looking? That steady gap came from somewhere, somehow.

Two shadows lie long across the grass. They move as one over the pale ground. The fur around its mouth has gone white. Its short legs carry it on, slow and sure. The wind stirs the bare branches above. A few dry leaves turn on the path. The pale sun gives little heat today. The cold sits deep in the wooden bench. The boy sits still and reads the two shadows.

To anyone else, it would look like nothing. A dog, a person, a walk in the cold. So plain that no one would look twice. But the boy keeps looking, and sees more. They move as one, and never need to look. That takes something he cannot see. He feels it now, behind the ordinary thing — a weight he has no name for yet.
