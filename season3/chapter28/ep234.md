A dull, cold afternoon settles over the park. The boy sits and takes them all in at once. The red light rests on the bench, calm and still. The small dog lies near, its chin on its paws. The reddish dog keeps his place to her right. The little tortoise waits by her feet, unmoving. The boy lets his eyes move slowly among them.

Each one holds a place, and the place fits. The small dog's is close, warm, and sure. The reddish dog keeps a step back, on guard. The tortoise stays low, at the red light's feet. No one chose these places today, he thinks. No one measured them, or set them out. They settled into shape on their own. The boy has watched them now for many days. He reads the gaps the way some read words. Each distance has a weight he can almost feel. Each weight, he senses, holds time inside it.

The thin light lies flat across the grass. Their shadows fall together, long and still. A cold wind passes through the bare trees. Dry leaves slide along the empty path. The whole quiet scene holds its careful shape. Nothing in it moves out of place. The light fades by a slow degree. The air grows colder as he sits.

Then the red light shifts on the bench, just a little. Without a glance, the small dog shifts too, and keeps its place. The reddish dog's ears turn, then settle. The tortoise does not stir at all. None of them looks, yet each one knows. They move around her like one quiet thing. The boy watches it happen, and holds his breath. This is the weight he has felt — here, in a single small movement.
