A raw, wet afternoon settles on the park. The boy sits with the grey notebook open. The shapes of people pass along the paths. His pencil waits above the page, patient. He watches, the way he has always watched.

By the gate, a shape drags its dog along. The dog is old, and slow on the wet path. The shape does not wait for it, or look down. The boy writes one short line. By the pond, two shapes laugh and throw stones at the thin ice. The ducks scatter from the cracking sound. He writes that too. The lines are plain and cold, like all the others. The page fills, the way his pages always fill.

Then, across the park, the red light stops walking. Her old dog, Dango, is slow on the frozen path today. She does not pull him on. She does not look away. She kneels down on the cold ground beside him. She strokes his white muzzle, and waits, and strokes it again. The reddish dog sits down to wait as well. No one hurries anyone. The boy's pencil comes down towards the page — and stops. There is no line for this. His lines are all one kind. They have always been one kind. He looks at the page, then at the red light. The pencil stays where it is.

The kneeling is a small thing. No one else in the park has even seen it. But the boy cannot look away from it. A shape dragged its old dog past the gate today. And one knelt on the frozen ground, and waited. Both are true. The page holds only one of them. He closes the notebook, slower than usual. Maybe grey was never the whole truth. The thought is small, and new, and it stays.
