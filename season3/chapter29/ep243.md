Two nights have passed since he began. The greenhouse is the same each time. Cold glass, the lamp, the quiet table. The boy comes when the park grows dark. He sits, and takes up the work again. Tonight, like every night, it fights him.

Nothing fits the way he hoped it would. A wire slips loose and will not hold. Two parts that should join stay apart. For a moment, he wants to push it all away. His hands go still over the cold, dead parts. The greenhouse is dark and quiet around him. Then he thinks of the red light in the park. He thinks of the bond he cannot explain — the easy closeness, the years he cannot see. The wish to understand it is all he has. And it is enough to move his hands. He picks the wire up again, and bends it, slow and patient.

A small part falls and rolls away. He finds it in the dark and brings it back. The lamp hums faint above the table. His tools lie scattered where his hands left them. A sigh leaves him in the cold air. The night deepens beyond the old glass. The frost creeps white along the panes. An owl calls once, somewhere in the park. His fingers ache, but he does not stop.

The work is hard, harder than he thought. And yet a strange calm settles in him. The trouble does not anger him at all. Each failure shows him one more wrong way. He is closer than he was last night. He sets the loose wire down, and breathes. There is no hurry here, in the dark. The boy bends close, and tries again.
