The next day, the boy is at his table. The red light is there too, near the door. She is back today, quiet, and watches him. The greenhouse holds the cold afternoon light. The wrong spot waits where she pointed it out. He looks at it a moment, breathes, then bends to it and begins, without a word.

He takes the small joint apart again. His hands are slow and careful now. He sets it the other way, as it should be. A wire slides back into its place. The parts meet, and this time they hold. He works in silence; she watches, silent too. He thinks of her voice from the day before. The way it dropped, low and certain. "Here is wrong," she said, so sure. There was something in those few words. Not just the truth of where to look. Something else lived under them, soft and deep. Was it sadness? Was it longing? Few names for feelings live in him. But the something was real; he felt it.

That night, rain comes softly to the greenhouse. The red light has gone home in the dusk. The lamp burns warm above the unfinished shape. Its pale eyes wait, dark and unlit. The rain runs in lines down the black glass. A slow drip falls from the old roof. The boy sits alone and does not work. He only listens, and turns the day over.

The something has begun to grow in him. From it, another feeling slowly comes. It is warm, and quiet, and will not leave. It pulls him towards her, soft and sure. He wants to know her — who she is. He wants to come back tomorrow, and build beside her. The boy sits with the rain, and waits for morning.
