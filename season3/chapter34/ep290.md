The years in the cave have their own slow speech. The man works. The machine grows. And every day, through it all, the two birds talk. They talk to each other, low and even, the way old soldiers do. And they talk to the silver one, though it has never understood. Pen-san tells it about the sea. The General reports the weather at the cave mouth. The words fall on it like snow, and slide away.

Then, one day, the words do not slide away. Pen-san is speaking of the spring ice, saying nothing important at all. And the silver one turns its head. It turns towards his voice, and its pale eyes wait, the way a listener waits. Pen-san stops. The cave goes very still. "You hear us now," he says, slow and careful. The blue-white eyes stay on him. They do not drift away to the ice or the man. They hold, and they listen.

The General comes closer, stiff with age. "Storm from the west by morning," he says, as a test. The silver one turns to him. It looks out at the dark sky beyond the cave mouth, and then back. The two old birds stand very still. After all the silent years, something has opened.

Pen-san settles down beside the silver one, slow and warm. There is so much to tell it. Where to begin, after so long? He begins with small things. The sea. The light. The long winters. The silver one listens, and the words no longer fall like snow. In the deep south, at the end of the world, something has changed. The loneliest knowing has found an ear at last.
