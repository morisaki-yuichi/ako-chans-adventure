The cave is dark now, and very quiet. The man sleeps at last, worn out by the sending. The machine rests, its lights low. And at the empty place on the ice, the two old birds keep the last watch of all. It is the place where the silver one used to stand.

"We said goodbye to it twice," says Pen-san, at last. His voice is low and rough with age. "Once, long ago, when it went ahead of us. And now, tonight, when it went back."

The General is quiet for a long time. "No," he says. "The second one was not a goodbye." Pen-san turns his head. The General keeps his eyes on the empty ice. "It has gone back to the start," he says. "To the garden. To her. Everything it was to us — all of that is still to come." He straightens, old soldier to the end. "That was not a goodbye. That was a hello."

Pen-san stands with this for a while, the way one stands with something large. Then he settles, slow and heavy, his feathers loose with age. "A hello," he says. "Then we did not lose it tonight." "No," says the General. "Tonight, it set out to meet us."

The cave holds the two small voices and lets them fade. The man sleeps. The machine hums its low, spent hum. Far away, and long ago, a garden lies dark under the last stars. Dew sits heavy on the grass. The two old birds turn and face the cave mouth, northward, into the dark. And somewhere at the beginning of everything, a small light is about to arrive.
