The years pass in England too. They pass gently, the way quiet years do. Summers fill the park with long green light. Winters strip the trees and frost the bench. Ako-chan walks the same path through all of them. The friends walk it with her, slower each year, and closer.

Dango's muzzle is white all over now. He sleeps through most of each afternoon, warm against her foot. The three tortoises have grown broad and calm. Suke-san still takes his place behind her, though his step has softened. Luca still crosses, now and then, to the far bench. She waits there a while, small on the grass, and then comes back. No one has sat on that bench for years. The park keeps it anyway.

At home, the garden corner by the shed stays bare. The house is full of slow, familiar sound. On the desk, the red notebook lies where it has always lain. Ako-chan dusts it, and sets it down again. She does not open it often now. But it is never far from her hand. Some evenings she rests her fingers on the cover, just for a moment. Then she goes on with the small work of the house.

Far to the south, at the bottom of the world, other years are passing too. She does not know what they hold. She only knows the shape of her own days — full, and warm, and quietly incomplete. A line is still waiting in the notebook. She is in no hurry, and neither is it. The years pass, the last line waits, and the two of them grow old together, quietly.
