The man's days find a new shape, after the sending. He wakes, and eats, and tends the quiet machine. He walks the shore ice for snow, alone now, without the pale light beside him. The cave is large, and the silence in it is larger. But he is not alone in it. He has never been alone in it.

The two birds go where he goes. Pen-san walks the safe path ahead of him, as he has for years. The General stands his watch at the cave mouth, morning and night. When the man eats, they settle near him, two old shapes in the gloom. When he sleeps, one of them is always awake.

"He thinks he is alone," says Pen-san one evening, watching him bend over his small fire. "He is not," says the General. The words drift past the man and are gone. He stirs his pot and hears only the wind. The fire snaps softly in the blue dark.

Something has changed in him, all the same. In the evenings, now, he says small things aloud to the birds. "Cold tonight," he tells them. The General said the same at noon, in words the man will never hear. "Storm by morning," the man says another day, reading the sky. Pen-san read it first, hours before. The man does not know he is answering them. But he speaks, and the cave is warmer for it. The boy who told no one anything now talks, a little, every day. He talks to two old birds at the end of the world. They listen. They always listen. And none of the three of them is alone.
