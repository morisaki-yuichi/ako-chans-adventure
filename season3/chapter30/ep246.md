The spring afternoon fills the greenhouse with light. The work moves between their four hands, easy now. The silver body is growing, panel by panel. Words pass when they are needed, small and plain. "Hold this." "Like this?" "Yes." The boy has learned the shape of speaking, a little.

Then a wire slips deep behind a panel. The boy needs her eyes, and her smaller hands. He turns to her — and stops, his mouth open. He has nothing to call her. The small dog has a name: Dango. Even the loud crow outside seems to have one. But the red light beside him has no word at all. In his head she has always been the red light. It was enough, before. It is not enough now. His hand hovers. He taps the table once, softly. She looks up.

She sees his stopped mouth, and she understands. "I am Ako-chan," she says, simply. "You can call me Ako-chan." The boy holds the sound. Ako-chan. A person's name, given to him, for keeping. She does not ask for his name. He does not offer it. The quiet takes that too, and holds it. She bends to the slipped wire, and frees it. The work goes on, as if nothing has changed.

But something has. That evening, walking home, he tries it once. "Ako-chan," he says, low, to the empty path. The word is warm in the cool air. All his life, people were shapes without names. Now, in all that grey, there is one name. The red is still red — he still cannot see her face. But the red answers to a name now. And the name is his to keep.
