## Overall Issues

### Story Development

**Day 41 spanning 22 episodes (Ep61–82)**

The 22-episode Day 41 is inherently a suspension of real-world time logic, but the series handles it well by framing the time travel as existing outside normal time. Three full "jumps" happen and return (Japan → North Atlantic → Bering Sea → return), each containing its own mini-arc, and then the Ice Age accident adds a fourth. The machine's interior scenes between jumps are short and functional. The reader is never asked to believe 22 episodes of normal action fit into one day — the time travel framing makes the compressed story_day logical. No fix needed, but a short note in the text somewhere (even as light as Obia saying something about time not passing "out there" while they were "in there") could reinforce this for careful readers. This is not a defect — just an observation.

**Day 41 → Day 42 transition (Ep82 → Ep83)**

Clean. Ep82 ends with Ako-chan closing her eyes and holding the four voices. Ep83 opens with her waking before the others. The day transition is handled naturally ("She woke before the others.") and the shift from completion to the unease of "Not yet" is well-motivated.

**Day 42 → Day 43 transition (Ep86 → Ep87)**

Clean. "They spent the next day inside the machine and in the cave." One sentence carries the transition.

**Day 43 → Day 44 transition (Ep88 → Ep89)**

"Morning came slowly" in Ep89. But Ep88 ends with Ako-chan sitting on the floor beside Obia's feet until "the machine grew light again" — meaning she sat there until the machine's own morning. Then Ep89 opens "Obia went to them in the evening." So Day 44 starts after Ep88's all-night vigil and ends with the evening of Ep89. This is internally consistent but readers may find the rapid day shifts (Days 42-44 in Ep83-100) slightly compressed. This is not a bug, but the farewell arcs (Ep87-96) all happen within Days 43-44 — about a day and a half of in-story time. This is fine emotionally (grief should feel compressed and intense) but the timeline skips from Day 45 (Ep97) to Day 50 (Ep98) without acknowledgment in the text.

**Day 45 to Day 50 jump (Ep97 → Ep98)**

Ep97 ends with the flying boat heading north. Ep98 opens "They came home to England." and then immediately "Days passed." The 5-day jump is handled adequately. The phrase "Days passed. Life found its shape again." does the work that a transition needs to do. The jump from Day 45 to Day 50 is 5 days of travel England-ward, which is plausible given the distance. This is satisfactory.

**Final arc resolution (Ep96–100)**

Satisfying. The disturbance thread is confirmed gone in Ep96 (at the cave) and confirmed again in Ep98 (at home in England), so the reader gets two confirmation moments. The "Who?" question in Ep99 opens the Season 3 hook cleanly without over-explaining. Ep100's "Obia——" is a perfect, restrained ending. The Season 3 bridge is implicit enough not to feel like a cliffhanger while clear enough to feel like an opening.

### Day Count Issues

**Day 41 spans Ep61–82 (22 episodes) — Design intention is clear**

The reason the day doesn't advance is that time travel operates outside the normal day count. The machine jumps, arrives in a historical era, the mission is conducted, and the machine jumps back — all of this happening within the same day in "real" time at the Antarctic base. This is implied but never stated explicitly in the episodes. A one-line acknowledgment from Obia (e.g., "When we jump, time here does not move.") would resolve any reader confusion. However, since the text is for language learners rather than hard-SF readers, the current approach (just showing Day 41 for all these episodes) is acceptable.

**Transition from Day 44 (Ep96) to Day 45 (Ep97)**

The farewell and departure happen on Day 44; the flying boat flies north on Day 45. This is consistent and makes logistical sense (Obia departs, machine disappears at the end of Ep96 = Day 44; the group boards the flying boat and departs on Day 45 = Ep97). No issue.

**Transition from Day 45 (Ep97) to Day 50 (Ep98)**

Ep97 ends mid-flight. Ep98 opens in England. "Days passed." bridges this. Five days of flight time to reach England is reasonable for the flying boat. The narrative gap is handled correctly with the time-skip summary paragraph.

**Timeline.md stale entry for Ep97**

As noted in the episode-by-episode section: `planning/timeline.md` line 2318 incorrectly lists Pen-san and General Adelie as being on the flying boat in Ep97. This needs to be updated to match the corrected episode (commit 5e24863).

### Difficult English

The series targets A1-A2 Japanese learners. The following items may be challenging.

**Past perfect tense used throughout (most significant issue)**

The writing guide specifies that Season 2 tense is: S1 (present, past, present progressive) + will + can/could. Past perfect (had + past participle) is listed as a *forbidden* construction under Season 1 rules, and Season 2 adds only will and can/could. Season 3 adds past progressive and present perfect, but still not past perfect.

Past perfect appears frequently in these chapters, including:
- Ep61: "Japan, Obia had said, before they came through."
- Ep68: "She had been on open seas before. She had crossed the Pacific."
- Ep70: "The voice the sea cow had given her."
- Ep73: "She had never seen him move that fast."
- Ep76: "He had been afraid — a little."
- Ep81: "Four animals who had been gone from the world for a very long time."
- Ep82: "The water had been there forever."
- Ep84: "She had known something was not finished. She had felt it."
- Ep89: "He had watched Obia since the first night in Antarctica." / "He had not known what Obia was."
- Ep98: "She had felt it for so long. She had felt it before she knew what it was. She had carried it."

This is pervasive and significant. However, a nuanced view: the past perfect in these episodes is used almost exclusively to express *reference back to something before the current scene* (= the classic pluperfect use), which is inherently harder to express in simple past without confusion. Many of these instances are genuinely difficult to rewrite without ambiguity. Given that the language level is A1-A2 (not strict A1), the occasional use of past perfect for clarity may be acceptable — but the writing guide prohibits it. This should be flagged as a series-level decision to make: either update the writing guide to permit limited past perfect in S2, or revise the episodes to eliminate it.

**Other vocabulary items to flag for A2 learners**

- "enormous" (Ep61, Ep78): within A2 range but not A1 core. Acceptable for S2.
- "ancient" (Ep75, Ep76, Ep79): same assessment — within A2, borderline.
- "straining" (Ep67): used in "something deep in them straining." May be unfamiliar. Consider "struggling" or "making a low sound."
- "recognition" (Ep76): abstract noun. The sentence "It was something like recognition" could be rephrased as "It looked at them as if it knew what they were."
- "vanishing" (Ep99): used in Ako-chan's recall of Obia's mission statement ("save the voices of the vanishing animals"). The mission statement phrase appears in italics as a recalled phrase (not narration), so it may be acceptable as a fixed proper expression from the story's mythology.
- "connection" (Ep84–90): used repeatedly as a technical term for the time machine link. Repetition is appropriate for comprehension at this level.

**Relative clauses**

Several complex relative clauses appear, notably:
- Ep66: "the way it stood on the rock — that shape Pen-san knew" (implicit relative)
- Ep73: "Gabu, who never moved before thinking, who had always watched and waited and counted" (stacked relative clauses)
- Ep76: "As if this creature — which had never seen a tortoise as small as them, from times it would never know — still understood what they were."

The Ep76 sentence is the most complex and contains nested elements (dash interruptions, relative clause, temporal phrase). For A2, this may be difficult. Suggested revision: "This creature had never seen a tortoise as small as them. It had come from a time they would never know. But it still understood what they were."

**Sentence length**

Most sentences are well within the S2 limit of 12-15 words. A few borderline cases:
- Ep67 line 17: "The Great Auk — that was what Obia had called them. The original penguin. They had lived here for thousands of years, in these cold waters, on these grey rocks. And then — not long after this moment — they would not." The last sentence, while short in word count, uses a dropped predicate ("would not [exist]") that requires inference. This is effective stylistically but challenging for A2 learners. Keep as is, or add: "And then they would be gone."
