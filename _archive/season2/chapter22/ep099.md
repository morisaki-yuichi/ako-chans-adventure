She stayed in the garden after the robin had gone.

She thought about Obia.

She thought about the machine — the great curved shape in the ice, the seam opening without sound, the pale light inside. She thought about the connection between two times. About the message Obia had carried since before it could remember: save the voices of the vanishing animals.

Someone had built that machine.

Someone had built Obia.

Someone had put Obia into the machine and sent it back — all the way to England, to the old shed, to the garden. To be there the morning a girl in red glasses first heard that something was wrong.

She had known this in some way for a long time.

But she had not said it plainly. Not in words, not directly. She had felt it below the surface — the way she had felt the thin thread under the ice. She had known without knowing.

Now she knew.

Someone had chosen her.

Someone had known she could hear the animals. Someone had understood what that meant — what it could carry, what it could do — and had made something to find her and go with her and help her do it.

Who?

She did not know.

The oak tree moved a little in the wind. One leaf came down very slowly and landed near her foot.

Ruka was on the flat stone beside Ako-chan's foot. She had been there since Ako-chan sat down — not moving, not hiding, just there, warm in the morning sun.

Dango put his nose against her hand.

"I know," she said quietly. "I am thinking."

He pressed closer. Ruka did not move. She stayed on the stone.

She looked at the sky. The light came through the leaves in long, slow pieces.

The question was there now. Clear and still and very large.

She did not have the answer.

But she had the question.
