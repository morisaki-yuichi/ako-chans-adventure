The shake passed.

The machine was quiet. Ako-chan looked at the others. No one had fallen. Ruka's yellow pod glowed steady in the corner. The green and orange pods sat still against the wall.

Suke-san looked up at her. She gave a small nod.

Then the seam opened again.

The air that came in was cold and wet — but different now. No trees. No wood smell. Only salt. Only wind. Only the sound of water on rock.

She stepped out.

A rocky island. Grey stone dropping away on all sides to dark water below. The sky was heavy with cloud. Waves hit the rocks and pulled back and hit again. Far away, through the grey, other islands were just shapes.

North Atlantic, Obia had said. The early nineteenth century.

The wind was sharp. Ako-chan pulled her coat close.

Then she saw them.

On the flat rock near the edge — a group of them, walking slowly with small careful steps. Black and white. Upright. Their wings were very short, folded at their sides. They could not fly. She had seen birds like this before, but not quite like these. Bigger. Heavier. Something about them felt very old.

One stopped and looked toward the group.

It did not run.

Behind Ako-chan, Pen-san made a small sound. Just one, very quiet. Not his usual sound — not happy, not excited. Something else. Something she had never heard from him before.

She looked back at him.

He was staring at the birds with very wide eyes, not moving.

General Adelie was at his side. Equally still.
