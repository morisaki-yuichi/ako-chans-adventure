On the fifth day, Ako-chan brought Ruka to the field.

She made a small warm corner for Ruka near the hedge, in the sun. Ruka settled there and did not move much. Just watched.

Then Ako-chan walked to where Obia was working.

She did not ask. She just stood and watched until she understood what Obia needed. Then she held the part in place.

Obia looked at her hands. It did not speak. It took the next piece and fitted it in.

They worked like that for a long time.

Obia did not explain everything. But it slowed when something needed to be held. And she learned when to hold and when to step back.

Dango sat at the edge of the field and watched. He was quiet. Not the way he was quiet when something was wrong — just the quiet of watching someone you trust.

Near midday, Obia stopped and looked at the section they had finished.

"Better," it said.

"I did not know what I was doing," said Ako-chan.

"You knew enough," said Obia.

She looked at the join. It was smooth and clean. She had not done the join — she had just held the piece while Obia worked. But it felt different from watching alone.

Ruka was still in her corner near the hedge. Eyes open.

Dango came slowly across the field and sat beside Ako-chan.

The flying boat was not finished. But it was starting to look like itself.
