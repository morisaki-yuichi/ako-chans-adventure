Three days later, Ako-chan arrived at the field early.

Obia was standing very still.

That was unusual. Obia was almost always moving — checking, adjusting, fitting one thing to another. When Obia was still, something was not right.

On the ground in front of it was a piece. Long and curved. It needed to go into the front section of the body. Obia had tried to fit it twice already and stopped.

Ako-chan stood beside Obia and looked at the piece.

She could see the problem. The curve was going the wrong way. Not because the piece was wrong. But because of the angle. If it came in from the other side, it would fit.

"If you turn it," she said.

Obia looked at her.

"Like this." She moved her hands to show the angle. "The curve faces the other way. Then it fits."

Obia looked at the piece. Then at the place it needed to go. It was quiet for a moment.

Then it picked up the piece, turned it, and pressed it in.

It went in cleanly.

Obia was still. Then it looked at Ako-chan.

It did not speak. But it looked at her for a little longer than usual. Not checking. Just — looking. As if it was adding something to what it knew about her.

She looked away at the rest of the flying boat. The two wide sections were almost finished. The body was long and smooth. She could see clearly now what it would be.

"What is next?" she asked.

"The left section," said Obia. "It needs two people."

She walked over.
