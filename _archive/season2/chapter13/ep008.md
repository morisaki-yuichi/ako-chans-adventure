The morning was early. The sky was pale in the east.

Ako-chan came downstairs before the light was full. She opened the back door.

Obia was still there. In its corner, near the old shed. Facing south. Its eyes were the same hard blue-white from the night before. It had not moved.

"Obia," she said quietly.

"I am here," said Obia.

"Is it still there? The signal?"

"Yes," said Obia. "Still there. Not bigger. Not smaller. The same."

Dango came to the door. He looked at Obia. Then he looked south. He sat down beside Ako-chan. His tail was still.

Ruka was asleep by the south wall. The garden was very quiet. The right kind of quiet.

Ako-chan looked past the fence, past the road. She could not feel anything from the south. Not now. The ground was calm. The hedgehog had told her so, just a few days before. The birds were at ease. The disturbance was gone.

But Obia was still watching.

"Is it the same as before?" she asked. "Like the old disturbance?"

"No," said Obia. "It is different."

"How?"

"Before, the sound came from everything," said Obia. "From the ground. From the birds. From the water. It moved through all things. This is not like that."

She thought about the animals before the journey. The hedgehog. The fox. The swallow. The barn owl. All of them had felt the same thing. All of them had pointed south. The disturbance had moved through every living thing it touched.

"And now?" she said.

"Now the ground is quiet," said Obia. "The old sound is gone. What is left is this. One signal. From one place."

The robin landed in the oak tree and sang once. Clear and short.

Dango pressed against her leg.

The summer light came in over the fence. Ruka's head appeared from the shell. One small foot forward.

Everything here was right. Everything was in its place.

But Obia kept watching south.
