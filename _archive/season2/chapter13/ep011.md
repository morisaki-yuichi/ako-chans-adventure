That evening, Obia was very still.

Ako-chan found it near the window. Its eyes were soft and far away, like it was listening to something very distant. Not the garden. Not the house. Something much further.

"Obia?"

"I am looking at my own data," said Obia.

Ako-chan sat down nearby. Dango came and settled beside her, his chin on her knee.

"All of the journey?" she said.

"All of it. England. Croatia. Jordan. Japan. The Pacific. The ice. I have been through all of it."

"Did you find anything?"

"No," said Obia. "Nothing in the journey data. But—"

It stopped.

"But?" said Ako-chan.

"There is something else," said Obia. "A different part. Older than the journey. I am trying to look at it."

"Can you?"

Obia did not answer at once.

"Something is in the way," it said. "I can see that the memory is there. I can feel the shape of it. But I cannot read it."

Ako-chan looked at Obia. At its still eyes. At its silver body, quiet in the evening light.

She thought about the ship. About the Colonel seeing Obia's data spread across the navigation table. Every reading, from England to Antarctica. The Colonel had looked at it all and said nothing.

Obia had always carried more than she knew.

"What kind of memory is it?" she asked.

Obia was quiet for a long time.

"I do not know yet," it said. "But it feels important. Very important. More important than anything in the journey data."

The evening light was long and warm across the floor.

Dango's ears moved. Forward, then back.

Obia looked south.

The garden was very still.
