On the second day, Obia told her more.

"The signal comes from far south," it said. "Very far. Further than England. Further than the sea. Further than the desert."

Ako-chan set down her pen. She had been writing in the red notebook. She looked up.

"How far?"

Obia turned its head south. "The same distance as before. The same place."

Something moved inside her chest. Not fear. Something older.

"Antarctica?" she said.

"Yes," said Obia. "The far south. The ice."

She was quiet for a moment. She thought about the ice. The white. The blizzard. The whales going under the water. The Colonel. Pen-san pressing against her leg while the aurora moved overhead.

"The same place," she said.

"The same place," said Obia. "But not the same thing. The disturbance before came from the ground under the ice. It spread north. It touched everything."

"I know," said Ako-chan. "I felt it."

"This signal does not spread," said Obia. "It stays in one place. Very deep. Under the ice. And it does not move. It just sends. Again and again. The same pattern."

Dango was at her feet. He had been there all morning. He pressed against her ankle and stayed.

Ako-chan picked up her pen. She held it over the page.

She did not write.

The south. Again. The same ice. But not the same thing.

She thought about Pen-san. About the Colonel in the dark. About the first time she had looked at the white line on the horizon and known they were close.

"Something under the ice is calling," she said.

Obia looked at her.

"Yes," it said.
