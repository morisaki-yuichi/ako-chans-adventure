Later that morning, Obia came inside. Dango was on the mat. Ruka had moved to a patch of sun near the window.

Obia stood at the table. Its eyes were the usual soft blue-white. But they were not still. They moved, very slightly, side to side.

"Tell me more," said Ako-chan.

"The signal repeats," said Obia. "The same pattern, again and again. A natural sound is not like this."

"How is it different?"

"A natural sound changes. It grows. It moves. It is alive." Obia paused. "A signal is made. Something or someone makes it. And it stays the same."

Ako-chan sat down with her hands around a cup of tea. "Something made it."

"I think so," said Obia. "Perhaps a long time ago. But it is still sending."

Dango raised his head. He looked at Obia. Then he put it back down.

Outside, a light wind moved through the oak tree. The leaves turned for a moment, showing their pale undersides. Then they settled.

"Why can we hear it now?" asked Ako-chan. "Why not before?"

Obia was quiet.

"I think it was always there," it said at last. "But before, the disturbance was louder. It covered everything. The signal was underneath. Now the disturbance is gone. And this is all that is left."

Ako-chan looked out at the garden. The roses on the fence were open. The sun was warm on the leaves.

The south was quiet now. All the animals had told her so.

And yet, from somewhere deep inside that quiet, something was still calling.

Ruka took one slow step toward the window. Then stopped.
