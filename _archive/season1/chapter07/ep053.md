The air grew colder as they moved north.

The sea changed too — darker, rougher. Small white shapes of foam moved on the surface. The sky was a deeper grey.

Ako-chan wore her jacket now. Dango's thick coat kept him warm. Suke-san had never looked cold. He walked the deck like the cold was not there.

This was the thing about Suke-san and Dango on the ship. The deck was not large. They both wanted to be near Ako-chan.

Dango sat on her left in the mornings. Suke-san sat on her right at meals. Dango lay at her feet when she wrote in the notebook. Suke-san appeared at the door of the cabin when she came out.

Neither said anything about it.

Daru noticed. He said: "Why do they both—"

Gabu said: "Dogs."

"But what are they—"

"Quiet," said Gabu.

It was not angry. Daru went quiet.

The ship moved north. The whales were always there — sometimes very close, sometimes far off. But always north of them. Always ahead.

At evening, Ako-chan stood at the front rail and watched the sea. Dango came and sat on her left. After a moment, Suke-san came and sat on her right — a little back, a little to the side. The same as always.

The three of them looked north together.

The water here was wild — big slow waves, cold air, the smell of something deep and clean and very far from land.

Ako-chan felt something in her chest: not fear. Not excitement. Something larger. Something she did not have a word for yet.

The whales called, far ahead in the dark.
