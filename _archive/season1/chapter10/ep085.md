The ice let go just before midday.

The scraping stopped. The hull was free. The ship moved into open water and slowed. Ice all around — but not pressing. Not closing in.

Ako-chan stood at the front rail. Her hands shook a little. Just her hands. She held the rail and did not move.

Suke-san came and stood beside her. Not a little to the side. Right beside her, his shoulder against her leg.

She put her hand on his back. He stayed.

The cold was strong. Ako-chan's suit kept her warm. Suke-san had his boots and his vest. He stood like the cold was nothing.

The blizzard was softer now. Snow came straight down, slow and quiet. Not the wall of white from the morning before.

Suke-san turned his head and looked back into the ship.

Obia was at the engine room door. It checked the numbers one more time. Then it walked to the navigation table. It put the data away — England, Croatia, Jordan, Japan, the Pacific, all the readings from every day of the journey — back in its place.

No hurry. No noise. Just the work.

Suke-san watched.

Obia did not look up. It did not check if anyone was watching. It did not explain itself or speak. It just did what it always did. The same way it always did it.

Suke-san's ears went back, then forward.

He watched Obia for a long time.

Then he turned back to the snow and the water.

He was still standing against Ako-chan's leg.

He did not move away.
