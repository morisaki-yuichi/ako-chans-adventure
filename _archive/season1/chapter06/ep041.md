After the mountains, the road went down to a small town in the west of China.

There was a train there. It went east. They got on.

For five days the train carried them through lower and lower land. Hills, then green fields, then wide rivers. The world outside the window changed slowly and steadily.

Then, one morning, Ako-chan smelled something new through the window.

The sea.

She looked up. Dango raised his head.

"Close," said Obia.

The train came into Shanghai. They got off and walked to the water.

It was a large port — ships and noise and boxes piled high. The sea here was dark grey-green, not the warm blue of the Greek crossing, not the cold dark water off Croatia. A different sea in a different part of the world.

Daru looked at it from the pod. "Is this the same sea?" he said.

"No," said Obia. "Different sea. East of here, it connects to the ocean."

"The ocean," said Daru slowly. He said it like a new word.

Gabu walked to the water's edge and looked out. He was very still for a long time.

A large boat was waiting at the far dock. White, with a blue line along the side.

"That one," said Obia. "It goes east. To Japan."

Ako-chan looked at the boat. Then at the sea. The water moved in long, slow waves. Above the port, white birds turned in wide circles.

She felt the disturbance here too. The south was pulling at everything, even this far away.

"Tomorrow morning," said Obia.

"Tomorrow," said Ako-chan.

She watched the birds. They turned and turned.
