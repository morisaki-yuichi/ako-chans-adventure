Then, in the afternoon, the sea came back.

She saw it first between two hills. A thin slice. Very bright blue. Like a piece of sky that had fallen into the land.

The train ran down toward the coast. White stone. Blue water. Old walls along the shore. Light on everything.

"Split," said Obia. "This is where we stop."

Split. A short, strong word. She said it quietly.

The train slowed. Red roofs came past the window. Stone streets. A tall tower near the water. Small boats in the harbor. People on the platform below.

She stepped off. Dango jumped down after her. Obia followed, quiet and light.

The air was different here. Salt and stone and something alive. She breathed it in slowly. Dango lifted his nose. He smelled everything. The sea. The fish. The old walls. His tail moved slowly.

This was Croatia. Not a name on a paper. Not a word Obia said. The stone was under her feet. The sea was right there.

She looked at the water. It was the same Adriatic she saw from Italy. But now she was on the other side.

The birds above the harbor moved in circles. Fast. Anxious. She felt them without trying.

Something is close, she thought. Very close now.

Dango sat beside her. Obia beside Dango. She looked back at Obia. It looked back at her.

She looked south. The coast went south. The water went south. Somewhere ahead — something was waiting.

She could feel it. Like a long thin thread. Pulling.

She took a step.
