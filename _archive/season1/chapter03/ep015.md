They changed trains in the early evening.

The station was big and busy. High roof. Long platforms. The night train came in at half past eight — dark green and silver, long and slow. It moved down the platform and stopped with a sound like a long breath.

"Platform six," said Obia. "This train goes east. Through the mountains. Into Croatia."

She said the word quietly to herself. Croatia.

They found their seats. The car was quiet. Dango settled at her feet. Outside: lights of the city, then streets, then dark.

The train went into the mountains.

She could not see them. It was too dark. But she felt them. The air grew heavy. The train went more slowly on the long slopes. Through the black windows she could see shapes — big and still and dark.

The train stopped once. Men in uniforms came through the car. They looked at papers. They did not speak much. Then the train moved again.

"Was that the border?" she asked.

"Yes," said Obia. "We are in a different country now."

She looked out at the dark. The same mountains. But a different country.

She thought about home. The garden. The oak tree. The robin. It was very far away now. Dango shifted and pressed closer to her.

Then she heard something.

Very faint. Through the glass and the dark and the movement of the train — many voices. Deep and far away. Like they came from inside the mountains. She had never felt anything like this before.

"Can you hear that?" she said.

"I cannot hear what you hear," said Obia. "But something is here. The animals know we are passing."

She listened. The voices were slow and careful. Something old. Something patient.

She did not sleep for a long time after that.
