"Left," said Ako-chan. "Turn left. Now."

Obia turned the ship. A massive wave rolled past on the right — the place where they had just been. It was very large. It would have hit them straight on.

"How did you know?" said Obia.

"The whales," said Ako-chan. "They told me."

She had to shout over the wind. But the whale voices were under all the noise — low and steady and clear. *Left. Past the wave. Good. Hold this direction.*

"Hold this direction," she told Obia.

"For how long?"

"Until they tell me to change."

The rain was in her face. Her glasses were covered in water. She took them off and put them in her pocket. She held the rail and listened.

A wave came from the left. The ship went up hard. Her feet left the deck for one second. She held the rail. She came back down.

"Small turn right," she said. "Just a little."

Obia adjusted. The wave passed behind them.

Suke-san was still right beside her. He made no sound. But when the ship went up very steep, his paw came forward and pressed against her foot. Just for a second. Then he pulled it back.

She did not look at him. She kept her eyes south.

"Are you afraid?" said Suke-san. Very quiet. Almost too quiet to hear.

"Yes," said Ako-chan.

"Good," said Suke-san.

The same word Daru said to her, long ago on the Dalmatian coast. But different now. More quiet. More real.

"Left again," she said.

Obia turned.

The whales were there. Big and dark and steady in the deep water. They were not going anywhere.

Neither was she.
