That morning, Obia called Riro to the side of the ship.

At the waterline, mounted on the hull, there was something new. Curved and smooth. Open at the front. About the size of a small sea otter.

"Get in," said Obia. "The ship will carry you. You do not need to swim. And when we reach the equator — this will keep you cool."

Riro looked at it. He looked at Obia. He climbed in.

He sat inside and looked out at the water moving past the opening.

"Okay," he said.

The whales went ahead.

The ship turned south and followed them. The sea was wide and very quiet. No land. No birds. No other ships. Just the whales, the water, and the long pale sky.

Ako-chan stood at the front rail. Dango sat on her left. Suke-san stood on her right — a little back, a little to the side. The wind came cold and steady against her face.

"How many days?" she asked.

"Many," said Obia. "The whales know the way. I will follow them."

She watched the three dark shapes ahead. They were still there. All three. Moving just under the surface — big and slow and sure. She listened. Their voices were low and deep and calm. Not words the way people use words. But clear. *This way. Come. We know.*

She trusted them in the north. They brought her here. She would trust them now.

Riro came up beside her. He checked his fur — one paw, then the other. Then he looked at the sea.

"Still very big," he said.

"Yes," said Ako-chan.

From inside, Daru: "How do we know we are going the right way?"

A pause. Then Gabu: "The sun. Look where the sun is."

A longer pause.

"Oh," said Daru.

A small sound came from his pod after that. Not a sad sound. Just a quiet one.

The two pods glowed through the window — Daru's warm green, Gabu's steady orange. She was glad they were safe in there.

She took out the red notebook and wrote:

*Day fifty-two. Heading south. The whales go ahead. We follow.*

She put the notebook away. The ship moved on. The sky was pale and wide. The south was somewhere below all this dark water. Still far. But closer than before.

She held the cold rail with both hands. The wind did not stop. Neither did she.
