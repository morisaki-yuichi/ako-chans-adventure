The boat left before dawn.

The port lights went away behind them. Then there was just the dark sea and the dark sky and the low sound of the engine below their feet.

They were on the water for two nights.

This sea was not like the warm Greek crossing. It was wide and grey and moved in long slow rolls. Sometimes Ako-chan held the rail and felt the boat rise and fall under her.

Dango did not like the rolling. He pressed himself flat on the deck, head low, eyes open. Ako-chan sat beside him and put her hand on his back. He pressed back against her palm.

Gabu sat in one spot and did not move. He had gone very still the way he sometimes did — watching something that most others could not see.

"What do you feel?" said Ako-chan.

"The water," said Gabu. "Under the surface. Something is moving in it from very far away."

"The disturbance?" said Ako-chan.

"Maybe," said Gabu. "I do not know what to call it. But it is moving through the water. I can feel it in the hull."

Ako-chan kept her hand on Dango's back. She looked east.

On the second night, she could not sleep. She stood at the front of the boat in the dark. The sea moved under her. The stars were very bright.

Somewhere ahead was Japan.

She thought: there is someone there. Waiting. But not the way Daru waited, out in the open. Not the way Gabu waited, still and counting.

Something different.

She did not know yet what.
