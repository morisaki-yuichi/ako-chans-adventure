The desert was behind them.

Two days east of Wadi Rum, they were on a bus going east. It moved all day through flat pale land. No red walls. No sand. The earth here was wide and brown and still. The sky above it was very large.

At a rest stop, the bus stood by the side of the road. Gabu walked out a little way and looked at the ground.

Daru watched from the pod. "It is so flat," he said.

"We left the desert," said Ako-chan.

"There is nothing here."

Gabu said: "Grass. Very short. Under your feet. But it is here."

Daru pressed his face to the wall of the pod. "Oh," he said. "Yes. I see it."

Dango trotted around them in a slow loop. His big fluffy tail moved in its long easy sweep. He breathed in and out. A new smell — dry and open and very old.

Back on the bus, Obia sat at the back. Ako-chan had noticed this: Obia always chose the back seat. Watching. Not the road. Not the animals. Something out to the south.

"Still there?" Ako-chan said.

"Yes," said Obia. "The signal. Steady. Not growing."

"But not stopping."

"No."

One bird crossed the sky outside the window — east to west. Ako-chan felt it pass: the same pressure, the same wrong note. Still there. Still coming from the south.

She took out the red notebook.

*Day twenty-three. Iran. Far east.*

That night the bus stopped at a roadside place. She woke once. The dark was very still.

Obia was bent over something on the ground, working by a small pale light. The same kind of work as the night before Wadi Rum.

She closed her eyes. The mountains were still days away. But Obia was already thinking ahead.
