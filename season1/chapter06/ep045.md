They came to Japan in the morning.

The coast grew out of the sea — dark hills covered in thick green. Not the pale limestone of Croatia. Not the red rock of Wadi Rum. This was a soft, deep green, right down to the water.

They came off the boat and walked away from the port.

Japan smelled different. Not like England, not like the flat land of Asia, not like the mountains. Here it smelled like pine and wet earth and something very old and very alive.

Ako-chan stopped and breathed in.

"What is it?" said Daru.

"The smell," she said. "The trees."

Daru sniffed through the pod's small vents. "Yes," he said. "Different."

Dango had stopped too. His tail was straight out. His nose was working fast. He tried to read everything at once.

Gabu looked at the tree line above the road. He was very still. Then he said: "The animals here are quiet."

"Quiet because of the disturbance?" said Ako-chan.

"Quiet," he said. "But listening. They know we are here."

Obia turned and pointed northeast. "There is something. Not a signal like the south. More like a pattern. An animal. Moving."

"Moving where?" said Ako-chan.

"Around us," said Obia. "A wide circle. Very careful."

Ako-chan looked at the trees. The green was thick and still. No movement she could see.

But she felt it: something close. Something alert. Something watching them from inside the forest.

Dango growled once — quiet and low. Then stopped.

"I know," said Ako-chan. "I feel it too."
