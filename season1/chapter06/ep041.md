The desert was behind them.

Two days east of Wadi Rum, the land had changed. The red walls were gone. The sand was gone. Now the earth was flat and pale and wide. It went on and on under a very large sky.

Gabu walked at the front. Slow and steady, the way he always walked. He looked at the ground just ahead of his feet.

Daru watched from the pod. "It is so flat," he said.

"We left the desert," said Ako-chan.

"There is nothing here."

Gabu said: "Grass. Very short. Under your feet. But it is here."

Daru looked through the clear wall and down at the passing ground. "Oh," he said. "Yes. I see it now."

Dango trotted beside Ako-chan. His fluffy tail moved in a long, easy sweep. He breathed in through his nose and out again. A new smell — dry and open and very old.

Obia walked at the back. Ako-chan had noticed this: Obia often fell behind the others and watched. Not the road. Not the animals. Something to the south. Always to the south.

"Still there?" Ako-chan said.

"Yes," said Obia. "The signal. Steady. Not growing."

"But not stopping," she said.

"No."

A slow wind came from behind them. Not warm, not cold. It just pushed them forward into the open land.

One bird crossed the sky high above — east to west. Ako-chan felt it pass: the same pressure, the same wrong note. Still there. Still coming from the south.

She took out the red notebook.

*Day twenty-three. East. Far east.*

She put it away. The road went on. Gabu led. Daru watched. Dango stayed at her side.

That night she woke once. The camp was dark. The flat land went away in all directions, very still under the stars.

Obia was bent over something near the ground. A small pale light moved slowly in Obia's hands. Careful work. The same kind of work as the night before Wadi Rum.

Ako-chan watched for a moment. Then she closed her eyes.

The mountains were still days away. But Obia was already thinking ahead.
