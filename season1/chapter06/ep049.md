They spent the day in the clearing.

Ako-chan told Suke-san about the journey. The robin in England. The swallow. The fox and the barn owl. Obia at the old shed. The train through the mountains into Italy. Daru on the rocky coast. Gabu in the desert.

Suke-san listened. He did not interrupt. He watched Ako-chan's face the whole time.

When she finished, he was quiet.

"The south," he said. "You are going all the way south."

"Yes."

He looked at the sky above the trees. Then back at Ako-chan.

Dango came and sat very close to Ako-chan — his shoulder pressing against her knee. Suke-san noticed. Suke-san noticed Dango noticing him notice it.

The two dogs looked at each other.

Dango sat very straight. His long coat spread wide around him. His big tail filled the space behind him.

Suke-san sat like a stone: compact and low and square and still.

Neither looked away.

Daru watched from the pod. "What are they doing?" he said quietly.

"Dogs," said Gabu.

Ako-chan did not try to stop them. She knew they had to do this.

After a long time, Dango looked to one side. Just once — not backing down, just done. Suke-san watched him for another moment. Then he looked at Ako-chan.

"Obia," he said. "I do not trust it."

"I know," said Ako-chan.

"That does not change. Not now."

"I know."

He looked out to the south. Through the trees. Into the sky. Something far away that only he could feel.

"The south is getting worse," he said.

"Yes," she said.
