Five days later, the flat land ended.

The road went up. First slowly, then more and more. The air grew thin and cold. The path turned narrow. On both sides, big grey peaks rose into low cloud.

Daru's pod hummed a little louder. Its warm orange light held steady inside the clear walls. Daru pressed his face to the front and watched the rock walls move past.

Gabu walked ahead. But here he moved more slowly than before. The ground was loose and the path was uneven. He chose each step with care.

"Do you know this kind of ground?" said Daru.

Gabu did not look back. "No," he said. "But I know how to walk on ground I do not know."

Daru thought about this. He said nothing more.

Dango moved close to Ako-chan. The cold did not bother him much — his thick coat held the warmth in. But the narrow path made him stay near her. His shoulder touched her leg as they walked.

Ako-chan breathed out. A small white cloud came and went in front of her face. Above, the peaks were lost in grey. A bird called once — one short clean sound from inside the rock face, and then it was gone.

She felt something in the mountains: high and cold and very still. Not the push from the south. Something older. These mountains had been here long before the disturbance began.

Obia said: "The path goes down on the other side. Three more hours."

"Down," said Daru. "Good."

They walked on. The wind came through the pass — cold and sharp. Gabu went forward, one step at a time. Dango stayed close.

The mountains did not move. The cloud did.
