After the mountains, the road went down.

Then east, and down again. Five days of lower and lower ground. Then, one morning, Ako-chan smelled it.

The sea.

She stopped walking. Dango stopped beside her. His nose pointed east.

"Close," said Obia.

They reached it at noon. It was a big port — ships and noise and boxes piled high. The sea here was dark grey-green, not the warm blue of the Greek crossing, not the cold dark water off Croatia. A different sea in a different part of the world.

Daru looked at it from inside the pod. "Is this the same sea?" he said.

"No," said Obia. "Different sea. East of here, it connects to the ocean."

"The ocean," said Daru slowly. He said it like a new word.

Gabu walked to the edge of the port and looked out at the water. He was very still. He stayed like that for a long time.

A large boat was waiting at the far dock. White, with a blue line along the side.

"That one," said Obia. "It goes east. To Japan."

Ako-chan looked at the boat. Then at the sea. The water moved in long, slow swells. Above the port, white birds turned in wide circles, calling.

She felt the disturbance here too — faint, from the east. The south was pulling at everything, even here, even this far from the start.

"Tomorrow morning," said Obia.

"Tomorrow," said Ako-chan.

She watched the birds. They turned and turned. The sea moved under them.

She thought: Japan. Almost there now.
