The ship moved very slowly.

The small otter watched them come. It did not dive. It did not swim away. It just floated, paws pressed to its chest, eyes open. Waiting.

Ako-chan leaned over the rail. She spoke very softly.

"Hello. You are safe. We are not going to hurt you."

The otter's eyes moved to her.

She felt it then — what the otter felt. A long time alone on the water. Cold that went all the way through. And something else: a current had picked it up and carried it far from where it knew. It did not know this place. It did not know which way to go.

"I know," said Ako-chan. "I know. Come here."

Obia had already lowered a flat platform from the side of the ship — smooth and wide, just at the water level. The otter looked at it. Then at Ako-chan. Then back at the platform.

It paddled over. Slowly. It climbed on.

Ako-chan reached down and put her hands around it — warm, round, surprisingly heavy for its size. She lifted it up.

It was soaked through. Very cold. Its dark brown fur was flat against its body.

Then it did something she did not expect. It pulled itself up against her chest, tucked its head under her chin, and stayed very still.

Dango made a small sound.

"It is all right," said Ako-chan, to both of them.

"What is its name?" said Daru.

Ako-chan listened. The otter's voice came small and tired: a name, or the shape of one.

"Riro," said Ako-chan.

Riro held on. Ako-chan held him back. The cold sea moved all around them.
