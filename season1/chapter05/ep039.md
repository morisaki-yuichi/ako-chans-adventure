All day, Gabu was in the rocks.

Ako-chan could see him sometimes — a small dark shape on a ledge, watching. She did not go to him.

Daru was less patient. He moved inside the pod and turned around. "He is still there," he said.

"Yes," said Ako-chan.

"He is just sitting."

"Yes."

"It takes a very long time. His way."

"That is Gabu," said Ako-chan.

Dango lay in the cool field with his nose pointed toward the rocks. He said nothing.

The day passed. The shadows moved across the red walls. Three desert birds flew over — south to north, very fast. Ako-chan felt them: the same push, the same wrong feeling from the south. Not smaller. No.

Near sunset the light turned orange and the shadows grew very long.

A movement in the rocks. The small dark shape, walking toward them. Slow. Steady.

Gabu stopped in front of Ako-chan.

"I have been here alone," he said. "Since the lizards left. Watching. Counting. Waiting for the pattern to be clear."

Ako-chan listened.

"The pattern is not staying here. It is moving north. Slowly. But it will not stop on its own."

"No," said Ako-chan.

"If I stay, I can still watch. But watching is not enough now." A pause. "I will come with you."

He said it the way he said everything — quietly, once, without looking away.

"We leave tomorrow," said Ako-chan. "Early."

Gabu turned and looked at the pod. Daru was watching him through the clear wall.

"Hello again," said Daru.

"Hello," said Gabu.

He turned and walked back to the rocks. To sleep one more night in the place he knew.
