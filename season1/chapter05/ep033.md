They came around a long curve in the road — and the rocks were there.

Ako-chan stopped.

Red. Not orange. Not brown. Red. Walls of stone going straight up from the desert floor. She tilted her head back to see the tops. Between the walls: pale sand, smooth and still.

The morning air was cool. They walked in.

By mid-morning everything had changed.

The heat came from above and below and from the red walls on both sides, all at the same time. Ako-chan's glasses slid down her wet nose. Each breath felt heavy.

Dango had stopped panting and was breathing in short, fast bursts. His tongue was very long. His tail was down.

Daru had stopped walking. His legs were pulled in. Not moving at all.

"Here," said Obia.

The rock face jutted out ahead and made a long shadow on the sand. Obia walked into it and stopped. The others followed.

The shadow was not cool. But it was better.

Nobody talked. Daru did not move. Dango lay flat with his chin on the sand. Ako-chan sat with her back against the rock.

Obia stood at the shadow's edge and looked at each of them in turn. It said nothing. Just looked.

Evening came. The heat eased a little. Then night came fast and the cold came with it.

Stars filled the sky above the red walls. Many more than at home, and very bright.

Ako-chan looked up at them. Then she closed her eyes.

A quiet sound, very close. She opened one eye.

Obia was bent over something on the sand. A small point of light moved slowly. Stopped. Moved again.

She watched for a moment. She did not ask. She closed her eyes.
