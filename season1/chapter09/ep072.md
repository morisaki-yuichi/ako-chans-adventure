Two days later, the horizon changed.

Ako-chan saw it first. A thin white line. Not water. Not sky. She blinked. It was still there. She pointed, and Dango barked once — short and sharp.

"Antarctica," said Obia. "We are very close now."

Nobody spoke. They came to the rail one by one and looked.

The whales had slowed. For the first time in many days, they stayed beside the ship instead of ahead. One on the left. One on the right. One behind. They moved with the ship now, very close. Their voices were quiet — not words, just a low, deep feeling. It came up through the hull and into Ako-chan's feet and hands.

Suke-san's ears were up. Dango stood very straight. From his pod, Daru pressed his face to the glass. Riro looked out from his capsule, then slowly went back in.

Gabu watched without a word.

From Riro's capsule, one small sound. Then quiet.

The pull from the south was not a pull anymore. It pressed from the air. From the dark water below. From the white line ahead. The voices of this place were old and very large. Not like England. Not like the Pacific. Something different and very deep.

She held the rail hard.

"How long?" she asked.

"One day," said Obia. "Maybe less. The current is with us."

The white line on the horizon was growing. Still far. But bigger than when she first saw it.

Dango came and sat beside her and pressed his shoulder to her leg. They looked out at the white together. Neither spoke. The white line did not stop growing.
