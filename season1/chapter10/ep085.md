The groaning went on all night.

Ako-chan did not sleep. She sat near the table and listened. Dango stayed at her side. Suke-san paced — three steps, turn, three steps — very quietly.

In the early morning, Obia said: "I know where the gap is."

The room went very still.

"There is a gap in the ice," Obia said. "To the east. About three kilometers. It will not stay open for long."

Colonel Adelie walked in from the corridor. "How do you know this?"

"The data," said Obia. "From the first day. I read the disturbance from England to here. Every reading. Every day." Its light touched the screen. "The ice is moving in the same pattern. It is opening to the east. Now. For a short time only."

The Colonel looked at Obia. "You carried this data all the way from England."

"Yes," said Obia.

"You did not tell us."

"There was nothing to tell," said Obia. "Until now."

The Colonel was quiet. Suke-san stopped pacing. He looked at Obia, then at the Colonel, and said nothing.

"Show me," said the Colonel.

Obia turned the screen toward him. All the data — England, Croatia, Jordan, Japan, the Pacific, Antarctica. Lines and numbers from every day of the journey.

The Colonel looked for a long time. He did not speak.

The ship groaned. The ice pressed.

"East," the Colonel said slowly. "You say east."

"Yes," said Obia. "East. But not for long."

Suke-san sat down. He looked at the screen. He looked at Obia.

He did not look away.
