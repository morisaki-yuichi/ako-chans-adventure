A long time passed.

The Meiolania did not come closer. Daru and Gabu did not move. The wind came and went through the dry grass.

Gabu counted — he could not help it. Seconds, then more seconds. He got past forty before anything changed.

The creature's head moved very slightly. Not toward them. Not away. Just: noticing.

Daru looked at the eyes.

They were dark and calm and very, very old. Old in a way that he did not have a word for. Not old like Gabu's slow and steady thinking. Not old like the limestone coast or the desert sand. Something older. Something from before names.

And the Meiolania looked back at him.

It was not unfriendly. That was what surprised him. He had thought, when it came through the dry grass, that it might be something to be afraid of. And he had been afraid — a little. He was honest enough to know that.

But the look was not a threat.

It was something like recognition.

As if this creature — which had never seen a tortoise as small as them, from times it would never know — still understood what they were.

Same shell. Same ground. The same slow way of moving through the world.

Gabu felt it too. Daru could tell — by the way Gabu's head tilted, just slightly. By the way his eyes went quiet.

Behind them, small and still: Ruka's shell on the red earth.

The Meiolania waited.

They waited.

The pale sky above them did not move.
