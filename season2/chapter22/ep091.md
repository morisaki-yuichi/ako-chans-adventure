The morning was still.

Obia had not moved from the machine's entrance. Everyone was there — in their places, in their quiet. But one was missing.

Ruka was on the floor, three steps from the yellow pod. She had pulled herself all the way into her shell. Legs in. Head in. Everything in. Only the shell itself was there — small and pale against the dark cave floor.

Daru went to her. He pressed his nose gently to the edge of her shell.

"Ruka," he said. Very softly.

No answer.

He stepped back. He stood looking at the shell for a long time. After a while, Gabu came and stood beside him. They stood there together — looking down at Ruka, then up at Obia.

Obia came forward. Slowly. It crouched near the shell and stayed very close.

The cave was quiet for a long time.

"You went to the oldest ground," Obia said. Its voice was very low. "The oldest ground there is. And you walked out to meet it. I do not forget that."

The shell did not move.

But then — very slowly — Ruka's front leg appeared. Just one, just a little way. It pressed flat against the cold floor.

Obia did not move.

The leg stayed there a moment. Then it pulled back in.

Obia remained still a little longer. Then it stood.

Daru and Gabu were still looking up at it. Neither of them spoke. There was nothing to say that those few minutes had not already said.

"Thank you," said Obia. "Both of you."

Daru opened his mouth. Then he closed it. Gabu moved one step closer to Obia's foot and stopped.
