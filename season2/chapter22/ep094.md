Everyone had said what they needed to say.

Or they had not said it. Both were the same now.

Ako-chan stood in front of Obia.

She remembered the first time she had seen it — a silver shape by the old shed, two small bright eyes in the early morning. She had told it no. She had not known what it was. She had not been ready.

But it had waited.

And then she had said yes, and everything had come from that.

So many mornings. So many nights on trains, on ships, on ice. So many voices she had heard and written and carried. So many times she had looked at Obia and found it there — steady, exact, always working, always beside her.

Now it was here.

And in a little while it would not be.

She looked at Obia. Obia looked at her.

Its eyes were the same blue-white she had always known. Clear and still. Two small lights that had not gone out from the very first morning to this one.

She reached out and put her hand on top of Obia's head.

Obia went very still.

She held her hand there. The surface was smooth and cool under her palm. Not fur. Not warm the way Dango was warm. But real — something she knew, something that had been beside her for a very long time.

"I will keep listening," she said. Quietly. "I will write all of it. I promise."

Obia said nothing.

She held her hand there for a long time.

Then she took it back.

Obia looked at her.

She looked at Obia.

There was nothing else.
