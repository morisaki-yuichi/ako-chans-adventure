They came home to England.

The garden was the same. The oak tree. The rosebush near the fence. The robin on its branch, watching. The same morning smell — cool and green and old.

Ako-chan stood at the gate for a long time before she went in.

Days passed.

Life found its shape again. Meals and walks and quiet evenings. Dango found his favourite spot by the window and lay in the same patch of light he always had. Ruka slept near the warm wall. Everything was the same as before — and also different, the way things are after something very large has happened.

One morning, Ako-chan sat in the garden.

She was not doing anything. She was just sitting. Dango beside her. The robin in the oak.

She opened herself to the voices. The way she always did — wide and quiet, nothing held back.

The hedgehog. Two pigeons on the roof. A small fox far down the path. The robin, near, in the oak tree above her.

She listened.

They were all there. Clear and real and close.

But the wrongness was gone.

She had felt it for so long — that thin pull from the south, that note under every other note, the thing that said the world was not sitting right. She had felt it before she knew what it was. She had carried it all the way to the bottom of the ice and back.

Now it was not there.

She sat very still.

Dango pressed his warm side against her knee.

The robin sang — one clean line — and the garden held still around it.

She breathed out.

It was gone.
