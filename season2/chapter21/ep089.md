Obia went to them in the evening.

Pen-san was sitting near the cave wall, looking at the ice. General Adelie stood a little ahead of him, at the edge of the blue light, very still.

Obia stopped in front of them.

General Adelie looked at Obia for a long time. His eyes were the same as they had always been — careful, taking everything in. He had watched Obia since the first night in Antarctica, back when Obia was still a question. Back when he had not known what Obia was. Now the question was answered.

"You came with a message," said General Adelie. "You carried it all the way. You did what you were built to do."

He stood a little straighter.

"Your duty is done," he said. "Well done."

Obia's eyes stayed on him.

Pen-san had not said anything. He moved very slowly and stood close to Obia — so close that his side touched Obia's leg.

He did not say anything.

He just stood there.

The cave was cold and blue and quiet. The ice held the light the way it always had, and probably always would, long after all of them were gone.

Obia looked down at Pen-san.

Pen-san looked up.

Neither of them spoke.

After a while, Pen-san sat down and pressed himself against Obia's side. He was heavier than he had been the first time they met. He had grown.

Obia did not move away.

They stayed like that for a long time. In the cold blue light of the cave. The machine waiting behind them, dark and quiet and very still.
