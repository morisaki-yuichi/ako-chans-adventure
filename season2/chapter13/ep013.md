Three days passed.

Each night, Obia worked in the garden. Ako-chan could see the small light from the window when she looked out before sleeping. Each morning, Obia was at the table.

On the third morning, Obia said: "I know one thing now."

Ako-chan put down her tea. "Tell me."

"The memory is real," said Obia. "It is not empty. There is something inside it. I can feel the weight of it. I know, in the way I know things, that this memory is very important."

"But you still cannot open it?"

"No. The lock is strong. I have never found a lock like this inside myself." Obia paused. "I think someone put it there. I think it was closed on purpose."

Ako-chan looked at Obia for a long time.

"Someone made you," she said. "Someone built you. And they closed this memory."

"Yes," said Obia. "That is what I think."

"Why would they do that?"

Obia's eyes moved south.

"I think the answer is not here," it said. "The signal and the locked memory — I think one needs the other. And I think the key is in the south. Under the ice. With the signal."

She was quiet. She thought about the ice. About the long groaning of the hull when it closed around the ship. About what they had found there. And what they had not.

Something was still there. Something that had not been found.

Dango pressed against her side. He was very warm. She put her arm around him.

Outside, Ruka moved slowly across the stones. One foot. Then another.

The signal was calling. Patient. Far away.
