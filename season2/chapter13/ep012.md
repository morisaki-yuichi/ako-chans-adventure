In the night, Obia worked.

Ako-chan woke once and looked out of the window. Obia was in the garden. Very still. And then — a small light. Not from its eyes. From inside. From somewhere deep behind the chest.

She had seen that light before. On the night train to Croatia, a long time ago. And again in Antarctica, with the Colonel standing in the dark, watching.

She did not go down. She watched for a moment, then went back to sleep.

In the morning, Obia was at the table.

"I found something," it said.

Ako-chan sat down. "Tell me."

"The memory is there. I am certain now. I can see its edge. But I cannot open it."

"Cannot open it?"

"It is like a door," said Obia. "I can touch the door. I can feel that something is behind it. But it will not open. Something is locking it. And I do not have the key."

Ruka came out from under the table. One slow step. Stopped. Looked up at Obia.

"A locked memory," said Ako-chan.

"Yes."

"And the signal from the south—"

"I think they belong together," said Obia. "The signal and the locked memory. One is the answer to the other. But I cannot say which. Not yet."

Dango came and sat beside Obia. He did not touch it. He just sat there. Close.

Ako-chan looked at Dango beside the silver dog. Neither of them moved. The morning sun came into the kitchen. The garden was warm and bright outside.

The signal was still there. Far south. The same steady pattern. Calling.

She got up to make tea. The water was loud in the kettle. The morning went on around them.
